Climate Change Impacts on Australian Weather

This line chart shows the trend in Australia's average surface temperature anomalies from 1850 to 2025, relative to a 1971-2000 baseline. The upward trajectory significantly accellerates after 1950, underscoring how climate change is driving warmer conditions across the nation, leading to more extreme weather events.

Fossil Fuels: The Primary Driver of Climate Change

Burning coal, oil, and gas releases greenhouse gases that trap heat in the atmosphere, intensifying global warming and disrupting Australia's weather patterns.

This waterfall chart tracks the annual changes in Australia's coal-fired power plant capacity from 2000 to 2025. The shift from additions to significant reductions reflects plant closures due to economic pressures and policy shifts, marking progress in reducing coal reliance but highlighting the need for faster transitions.

This stacked bar chart illustrates the evolution of Australia's electricity generation mix from 1998-99 to 2024. While renewables have grown to 36%, fossil fuels still dominate at 65%, indicating that the pace of change is insufficient to meet 2035 emissions targets and avoid worsening climate impacts.

The Deadly Consequences of Climate Change

Rising temperatures and extreme weather events pose severe risks to human health, ecosystems, and infrastructure across Australia.

This heatmap displays the percentage of days each year hotter than the 90th percentile compared to a 1961-1990 baseline. The intensifying red shades in recent decades show a surge in hot days, which exacerbate health issues like heatstroke and strain vulnerable populations.

This symbol map plots Victorian bushfires by decade from 1925-2025, with triangle sizes representing burned area. The clustering and increasing scale in recent decades demonstrate how drier, hotter conditions from climate change are fueling more intense and frequent fires.

We must act urgently to reverse climate change by accelerating the shift to renewables, reducing emissions, and implementing strong policies. If we fail, Australia faces escalating threats: more devastating bushfires, prolonged heatwaves, severe droughts, and rising sea levels, endangering lives, economies, and our unique environment.
